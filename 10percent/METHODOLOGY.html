1. Overview

This analysis identifies the Top 10% wealthiest ZIP codes in the United States using two complementary scoring systems:

  • Household Geometric Score: Based on IRS wealth data, travel time, and high-income household concentration
  • Corporate Geometric Score: Based on business revenue, employment, productivity, and geographic location
Key Principle: Both scores use a Weighted Geometric Mean rather than arithmetic mean. This ensures that a ZIP code must perform well across ALL factors to achieve a high score - excellence in one area cannot compensate for poor performance in another.

5. Normalization Process

Min-Max Normalization:

Normalized_Value = (Value - Min) / (Max - Min)
Global Normalization: All values are normalized across the ENTIRE dataset (not per city). This ensures cross-city comparability - a score of 15 in New York means the same as a score of 15 in Los Angeles.

7. Intersection Analysis

The intersection identifies ZIP codes that appear in BOTH:

  • Top 10% Household Wealth
  • Top 10% Corporate Power
Intersection Percentage:

% of City = (Intersection ZIPs / Total City ZIPs) √ó 100

Interpretation

  • High % of City: The city has strong overlap between wealthy residents and corporate power
  • High % of HH Top 10%: Wealthy households tend to be in corporate-heavy areas
  • High % of Corp Top 10%: Corporate centers tend to be in wealthy residential areas
Key Finding: Houston and Dallas show the highest % of Household Top 10% intersected (67-71%), indicating wealthy households are strongly concentrated near corporate centers. San Francisco shows the lowest (22%), suggesting more geographic separation between residential wealth and corporate activity.

Why Distance Squared?

The distance is squared in both scores to emphasize geographic exclusivity:

  • For Households: Longer travel times indicate more exclusive, harder-to-reach locations
  • For Corporate: Greater distances indicate business activity spread across the metro area, not just near airports
